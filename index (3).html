Unit I — Introduction

Preamble to the Indian Constitution

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Introduction

The Preamble is the opening statement of the Constitution of India. It declares the ideals, objectives, and basic philosophy upon which the Constitution is founded. Adopted on 26 November 1949, it came into force on 26 January 1950. The words 'Socialist', 'Secular' and 'Integrity' were inserted by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976.

2. Text of the Preamble

WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC and to secure to all its citizens: JUSTICE, social, economic and political; LIBERTY of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship; EQUALITY of status and of opportunity; and to promote among them all FRATERNITY assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation; IN OUR CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY this twenty-sixth day of November, 1949, do HEREBY ADOPT, ENACT AND GIVE TO OURSELVES THIS CONSTITUTION.

3. Keywords Explained

TermMeaning
SovereignIndia is fully independent — not subject to any external authority.
SocialistAdded by 42nd Amendment (1976). Mixed economy — public and private sectors. Equitable distribution of wealth.
SecularAdded by 42nd Amendment (1976). No official state religion. All religions treated equally.
DemocraticGovernment derives authority from the will of the people through free and fair elections.
RepublicHead of State (President) is elected, not hereditary.
JusticeSocial (eliminate inequalities), Economic (equitable distribution), Political (equal political rights).
LibertyFreedom of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship — guaranteed through Fundamental Rights.
EqualityEquality of status and opportunity — no discrimination on any ground.
FraternityBrotherhood among all citizens — dignity of individual and unity of the Nation.

4. Is the Preamble Part of the Constitution?

(a) Berubari Case, 1960 — NOT Part

The Supreme Court held that the Preamble is not a part of the Constitution. It is merely a key to understanding the intentions of the framers but cannot be the source of any substantive power.

(b) Kesavananda Bharati, 1973 — IS Part

The Supreme Court overruled the Berubari decision and held that the Preamble IS a part of the Constitution.

⚖ Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala
AIR 1973 SC 1461
Held
Parliament has wide amending power under Art. 368 but cannot alter or destroy the Basic Structure of the Constitution. The Preamble is part of the Constitution and reflects its basic features.

5. Amendability of the Preamble

The Preamble can be amended under Article 368, but the amendment must not destroy the basic structure. The 42nd Amendment Act, 1976 is the only amendment to the Preamble so far — inserting 'Socialist', 'Secular', and 'Integrity'.

6. Significance of the Preamble

  • It is the soul and spirit of the Constitution.
  • It is the guiding star for interpreting constitutional provisions.
  • It declares the source of authority — "We, the People of India".
  • It sets out the goals: Justice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity.
  • Courts use it to interpret ambiguous provisions of the Constitution.

Unit I — Introduction

Nature of the Indian Constitution

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Introduction

The Indian Constitution is unique — it is neither purely federal nor purely unitary, but a blend of both. It is the longest written constitution in the world, framed by the Constituent Assembly (1946–1949) and enacted on 26 November 1949.

2. Key Characteristics

Written and Enacted Constitution

Originally had 395 Articles, 8 Schedules, 22 Parts. Today has 448 Articles, 12 Schedules, 25 Parts. Not evolved through custom (like Britain) but deliberately enacted.

Blend of Rigidity and Flexibility

Some provisions amended by simple majority (e.g., Art. 3 — creating new states). Others require special majority — 2/3rd of members present and voting + majority of total membership. Certain provisions also need ratification by at least half the state legislatures.

Federal with Unitary Bias

India has dual polity (Union and States) but strong unitary features — single citizenship, integrated judiciary, emergency provisions. K.C. Wheare described India as 'quasi-federal.'

Parliamentary Democracy

India follows the Westminster model. The Executive is responsible to the Legislature. Real power lies with the Prime Minister and Council of Ministers.

Independent Judiciary and Judicial Review

The Supreme Court is the guardian of the Constitution. Judicial review is a basic feature (Kesavananda Bharati, 1973).

Universal Adult Franchise

Every citizen above 18 years of age can vote regardless of religion, caste, sex, or literacy.

3. Sources of the Indian Constitution

SourceProvisions Borrowed
Govt. of India Act, 1935Federal structure, Judiciary, Emergency provisions, Governor, PSC
British ConstitutionParliamentary democracy, Cabinet system, Rule of Law, Bicameralism
USA ConstitutionFundamental Rights, Judicial Review, Independent Judiciary, Impeachment
Irish ConstitutionDirective Principles of State Policy
Canadian ConstitutionStrong Centre, Residuary powers with Centre
Australian ConstitutionConcurrent List, Freedom of trade and commerce
USSR ConstitutionFundamental Duties, Socialist ideals
South AfricaAmendment procedure
FranceRepublic, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity concepts

Unit I — Introduction

Characteristics of Federalism in India

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Introduction

Federalism is a system where power is divided between a central authority and constituent units (states). India has adopted a modified federal structure — maintaining unity while allowing regional autonomy.

2. Federal Features

(a) Dual Polity

Two levels of government: Union Government at national level and State Governments at state level, each with separate Executives, Legislatures, and Judiciaries.

(b) Division of Powers — Three Lists (Seventh Schedule)

  • Union List (List I) — 100 subjects: Defence, Foreign Affairs, Railways, Currency. Parliament has exclusive power.
  • State List (List II) — 61 subjects: Police, Public Health, Agriculture. State Legislatures legislate.
  • Concurrent List (List III) — 52 subjects: Education, Marriage, Forests. Both can legislate; Parliament prevails in conflict.
  • Residuary Powers: All unlisted subjects vest with Parliament.

(c) Supremacy of the Constitution

All laws inconsistent with the Constitution are void (Art. 13). Both Union and States must act within constitutional limits.

(d) Independent Judiciary — Arbiter of Federal Disputes

The Supreme Court has original jurisdiction in disputes between Union and States inter se (Art. 131).

3. Non-Federal / Unitary Features

  • Single Constitution for Union and States.
  • Single Integrated Judiciary — no separate state courts.
  • Single Citizenship.
  • All-India Services (IAS, IPS) — recruited by Union, serve in states.
  • Emergency provisions (Art. 352, 356, 360) — convert federal structure to unitary.
  • Governor appointed by President — Centre's representative in states.
  • Residuary powers with Centre.
⚖ S.R. Bommai v. Union of India
AIR 1994 SC 1918
Held
Federalism is a basic feature of the Constitution. Presidential proclamation under Art. 356 is subject to judicial review. Floor test must precede dismissal of state government.

4. Cooperative Federalism

India has evolved towards cooperative federalism — Union and States collaborate. Instruments: Finance Commission (Art. 280), Inter-State Council (Art. 263), GST Council, NITI Aayog.

Unit I — Introduction

Unitary Form of Government

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Meaning

In a unitary government, all powers are concentrated in a single central authority. Regional units derive their powers from the centre and remain subordinate to it. Examples: United Kingdom, France, Japan, Sri Lanka.

2. Key Features

  • Concentration of all powers at the centre.
  • No constitutional division of powers between centre and regions.
  • Local governments are creations of the central government — can be created, altered, or abolished.
  • Single, unified constitution — usually flexible.
  • Single citizenship and integrated judiciary.

3. Unitary Features in Indian Constitution

(a) Strong Centre

Union List has more and more important subjects than State List. In Concurrent List, Parliament's law prevails. Residuary powers rest with Centre.

(b) Emergency Provisions

  • National Emergency (Art. 352): Parliament can legislate for states; state Executive comes under Union control.
  • President's Rule (Art. 356): Central government takes over state administration.
  • Financial Emergency (Art. 360): Centre can give financial directions to states.

(c) Single Citizenship, Integrated Judiciary, All-India Services

Only one citizenship — Indian. One unified judicial system with Supreme Court at apex. IAS, IPS, IFS officers recruited and controlled by Union, serve in state cadres.

(d) Governor — Centre's Agent

Governors appointed by President. Can reserve state bills for Presidential consideration and report on breakdown of constitutional machinery.

4. Federal vs. Unitary Comparison

BasisFederalUnitary
PowersDivided between Centre and StatesConcentrated at Centre
SupremacyConstitution is supremeCentral government is supreme
State AutonomyConstitutional guaranteeNo guaranteed autonomy
JudiciaryDual / federal court systemSingle integrated judiciary
ExampleUSA, Australia, GermanyUK, France, Japan

5. India's Position

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: "Federal in structure but unitary in spirit." K.C. Wheare: "Quasi-federal." The Indian Constitution is essentially unitary in emergencies and federal in normal times.

Unit II — Citizenship & Fundamental Rights

Citizenship

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Meaning

Citizenship is the legal bond between a person and a state. A citizen enjoys full civil and political rights — right to vote, hold public office, and receive diplomatic protection abroad. India provides for single citizenship for all persons.

2. Constitutional Provisions (Articles 5–11)

ArticleProvision
Art. 5Citizenship at commencement — domicile in India + birth/descent/5-year residency.
Art. 6Rights of persons migrated from Pakistan to India.
Art. 7Persons who migrated to Pakistan — not automatically citizens.
Art. 8Persons of Indian origin residing outside India.
Art. 9Voluntarily acquiring foreign citizenship — ceases to be Indian citizen.
Art. 10Continuance of citizenship — subject to Parliament's law.
Art. 11Parliament empowered to regulate citizenship — the Citizenship Act, 1955.

3. Modes of Acquiring Citizenship (Citizenship Act, 1955)

(a) By Birth

Born in India on/after 26 Jan 1950 but before 1 July 1987 — automatic citizen. After 1987, at least one parent must be Indian. After 3 Dec 2004, both parents must be citizens, or one citizen and other not an illegal migrant.

(b) By Descent

Born outside India — citizen if either parent was an Indian citizen at time of birth (after 2003 amendment). Must be registered with an Indian consulate.

(c) By Registration

Available to persons of Indian origin residing in India for 7 years, spouses of Indian citizens (7 years), and minor children of Indian citizens.

(d) By Naturalisation

A foreigner who has resided in India for 11 years (including 12 months immediately before application). Must be of good character and have knowledge of an Indian language.

(e) By Incorporation of Territory

When a new territory joins India (e.g., Goa 1961, Sikkim 1975), the people of that territory become Indian citizens.

4. Loss of Citizenship

  • Renunciation: Voluntary act of giving up citizenship.
  • Termination: Automatic loss when voluntarily acquiring citizenship of another country.
  • Deprivation: Government can deprive for fraud, disloyalty, or trading with enemy during war.

The State (Article 12)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Why is "State" Important?

Fundamental Rights are guaranteed against the State. If an entity is not 'the State', a citizen cannot file a writ petition against it for violation of Fundamental Rights. Therefore, defining 'State' under Art. 12 is critically important.

2. Article 12 — Definition of State

"In this Part, unless the context otherwise requires, the State includes the Government and Parliament of India and the Government and the Legislature of each of the States and all local or other authorities within the territory of India or under the control of the Government of India."

3. Components of 'State'

  • Government and Parliament of India — President, PM, Council of Ministers, Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha.
  • Government and Legislature of each State — Governor, CM, Council of Ministers, State Legislature.
  • All local authorities — Municipal Corporations, Panchayats, District Boards, Port Trusts.
  • 'Other authorities' — expansively interpreted by courts (see below).

4. Judicial Interpretation of 'Other Authorities'

⚖ Rajasthan State Electricity Board v. Mohan Lal
AIR 1967 SC 1857
Held
'Other authorities' includes all authorities created by statute on whom powers are conferred by law. Statutory authority with power backed by sanctions qualifies as 'State.'
⚖ Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib
AIR 1981 SC 487
Tests for 'State' (Instrumentality/Agency)
(1) Financial resources provided by government; (2) Deep and pervasive government control; (3) Functions of governmental nature; (4) Government transferred a department to it; (5) Monopoly status conferred by state. If satisfied, the body is 'State.'

5. Private Entities

Purely private entities — even if performing public functions — are generally not 'the State' under Art. 12. However, if they receive heavy government funding and are under deep government control (Ajay Hasia tests), they may be treated as 'State.'

Fundamental Rights — Right to Equality (Art. 14–18)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Overview

The Right to Equality is the first and foremost Fundamental Right. It covers Articles 14 to 18 and is the cornerstone of the entire edifice of Fundamental Rights in Part III of the Constitution.

2. Article 14 — Equality Before Law

The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or equal protection of the laws within the territory of India.

  • Equality before law (British concept): Absence of any special privileges. No one is above the law.
  • Equal protection of laws (American concept): Like must be treated alike. Equal treatment in equal circumstances.
⚖ E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu
AIR 1974 SC 555
Held
Equality is a dynamic concept. Arbitrariness and inequality go hand in hand — any action that is arbitrary, unreasonable, or capricious violates Art. 14. Introduced the 'Doctrine of Arbitrariness.'

3. Article 15 — Prohibition of Discrimination

The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Exceptions permit special provisions for women, children (Art. 15(3)), backward classes and SC/ST (Art. 15(4)), educational institutions (Art. 15(5)), and EWS reservation (Art. 15(6)).

4. Article 16 — Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment

All citizens have equality of opportunity in public employment. Exceptions: reservations for backward classes (Art. 16(4)), promotion reservations for SC/ST (Art. 16(4A)).

⚖ Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (Mandal Case)
AIR 1993 SC 477
Held
27% OBC reservation upheld. However: (1) Total reservation cannot exceed 50%; (2) Creamy layer among OBCs must be excluded.

5. Article 17 — Abolition of Untouchability

'Untouchability' is abolished in any form. The Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955 and the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 give effect to this provision.

6. Article 18 — Abolition of Titles

The State shall not confer titles (other than military or academic distinctions). No citizen shall accept titles from a foreign state. Awards like Bharat Ratna and Padma awards are not 'titles' — upheld by the Supreme Court.

Freedom, Social Control & Personal Liberty (Art. 19–22)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Article 19 — Six Freedoms

Article 19 guarantees six freedoms to citizens only (not aliens). All can be restricted by the State on specified grounds.

RightGrounds for Restriction
Art. 19(1)(a): Freedom of Speech and ExpressionSovereignty, security of state, public order, decency, morality, contempt of court, defamation
Art. 19(1)(b): Freedom of peaceful assemblySovereignty, public order

2. Article 20 — Protection in Respect of Conviction

  • No ex post facto laws: No conviction for an act that was not an offence when committed.
  • No double jeopardy: No prosecution for the same offence more than once.
  • No self-incrimination: No person compelled to be a witness against himself.

3. Article 21 — Protection of Life and Personal Liberty

No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law.

⚖ Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India
AIR 1978 SC 597
Held
The procedure under Art. 21 must be just, fair, and reasonable. Art. 14, 19, and 21 are interconnected — the "Golden Triangle." Any law depriving life/liberty must satisfy all three articles.

4. Changing Dimensions of Personal Liberty — Implied Rights under Art. 21

  • Right to livelihood — Olga Tellis v. BMC (1985)
  • Right to health and medical care — Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity (1996)
  • Right to education — Unni Krishnan (1993) (now Art. 21A)
  • Right to speedy trial — Hussainara Khatoon (1979)
  • Right to legal aid — M.H. Hoskot (1978)
  • Right to privacy — Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (2017)
  • Right to shelter — Chameli Singh (1996)
  • Right to die with dignity (passive euthanasia) — Common Cause (2018)
  • Right to clean environment — Subhash Kumar (1991)

5. Article 21A — Right to Education (Added by 86th Amendment, 2002)

The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children aged 6 to 14 years. The Right to Education Act, 2009 gives effect to this.

6. Article 22 — Protection Against Arbitrary Arrest

  • Must be informed of the grounds of arrest.
  • Right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of choice.
  • Must be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours.
  • No detention beyond 24 hours without magistrate's order.

Note: These protections do NOT apply to enemy aliens and persons detained under preventive detention laws.

Cultural and Educational Rights (Art. 29–30)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Introduction

Articles 29 and 30 protect the rights of minorities — both religious and linguistic — to conserve their distinct culture and establish educational institutions. These rights are essential for India's pluralistic character.

2. Article 29 — Protection of Interests of Minorities

Any section of citizens having a distinct language, script, or culture shall have the right to conserve the same. No citizen shall be denied admission into any State-aided educational institution on grounds only of religion, race, caste, or language.

  • Art. 29 protects both minorities AND majority groups — it applies to any 'section of citizens.'
  • The right to conserve culture implies the right to preserve language and script.

3. Article 30 — Right of Minorities to Establish Educational Institutions

All minorities, whether based on religion or language, shall have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice. The State shall not discriminate in granting aid on the ground that the institution is under minority management.

4. Art. 29 vs. Art. 30

Right to establish educational institutions is a fundamental right. Linguistic minorities determined state-wise. State can regulate to maintain standards but cannot interfere with right to administer.

Right to Constitutional Remedies (Art. 32)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Article 32 — Heart and Soul of the Constitution

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar described Article 32 as "the heart and soul of the Constitution." It guarantees the right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. The right itself is a Fundamental Right — cannot be suspended except during a national emergency (Art. 359).

2. The Five Constitutional Writs

(a) Habeas Corpus — "To have the body"

Issued commanding production of a detained person before the Court. Used to secure release from illegal detention. Cannot be issued for lawful detention or detention outside India.

(b) Mandamus — "We command"

Issued to a public authority commanding it to perform a public duty it has refused or neglected to perform. Cannot be issued against the President, Governor, or private individuals.

(c) Prohibition — "To forbid"

Issued by a superior court to an inferior court/tribunal preventing it from exceeding its jurisdiction. Preventive in nature. Lies only against judicial/quasi-judicial bodies.

(d) Certiorari — "To be certified"

Issued to quash an order of an inferior court/tribunal when it has acted without or in excess of jurisdiction, or in violation of natural justice. Both preventive and curative.

(e) Quo Warranto — "By what authority"

Issued to a person asking by what authority they hold a public office. Can be sought by any person — not just the aggrieved party.

3. Article 32 vs. Article 226

BasisArticle 32 (Supreme Court)Article 226 (High Court)
ScopeOnly Fundamental RightsFR + any other legal right
TerritoryWhole of IndiaWithin HC's territorial jurisdiction
NatureItself a Fundamental RightConstitutional right (can be restricted)
SuspensionOnly during National Emergency (Art. 359)Cannot be fully suspended
⚖ Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar
AIR 1979 SC 1360
Held
Right to speedy trial is a fundamental right under Art. 21. Thousands of undertrials detained for longer than maximum sentence were ordered to be released. One of the earliest PIL judgments.

Directive Principles of State Policy (Art. 36–51)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Introduction

DPSPs are contained in Part IV (Articles 36–51). Inspired by the Irish Constitution of 1937, DPSPs are guidelines to the State for creating a just socio-economic order. They are non-justiciable but fundamental in governance (Art. 37).

2. Classification of DPSPs

(a) Socialistic Principles

  • Art. 38: Promote welfare — social, economic, and political justice.
  • Art. 39: Adequate means of livelihood, equitable distribution of resources, equal pay for equal work.
  • Art. 39A: Equal justice and free legal aid.
  • Art. 41: Right to work, education, and public assistance.
  • Art. 42: Just and humane conditions of work and maternity relief.
  • Art. 43: Living wage for workers.
  • Art. 47: Improve nutrition and standard of living; prohibit intoxicants.

(b) Gandhian Principles

  • Art. 40: Organise village panchayats.
  • Art. 43B: Promote cooperative societies.
  • Art. 46: Promote educational and economic interests of SCs, STs, and weaker sections.
  • Art. 48: Organise agriculture and animal husbandry; prohibit cow slaughter.

(c) Liberal-Intellectual Principles

  • Art. 44: Uniform Civil Code for citizens.
  • Art. 45: Early childhood care for children below 6 years.
  • Art. 48A: Protect environment, forests, and wildlife.
  • Art. 50: Separate judiciary from executive in public services.
  • Art. 51: Promote international peace and security.

3. Significance

  • Represent the welfare state ideal — positive obligations on government.
  • Basis for landmark legislations — Land Reform Acts, Maternity Benefit Act, Equal Remuneration Act.
  • Courts use them to expand the scope of Art. 21.
  • They are enforceable through political accountability even if not legally enforceable.

Inter-relationship: Fundamental Rights & DPSPs

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. The Conflict

Fundamental Rights (justiciable, negative obligations on State) and DPSPs (non-justiciable, positive obligations on State) conflict when the State enacts laws to implement DPSPs that restrict Fundamental Rights.

2. Constitutional Evolution

Phase I — Fundamental Rights Over DPSPs (1950–1967)

In State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951), Fundamental Rights prevail over DPSPs. Parliament responded with the First Amendment, 1951 (inserting Art. 15(4)).

Phase II — Parliament vs. Judiciary (1967–1973)

In Golak Nath (1967), the Supreme Court held Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights. Parliament retaliated with 24th and 25th Constitutional Amendments. Art. 31C inserted — laws implementing Art. 39(b) and (c) cannot be challenged under Art. 14, 19, or 31.

Phase III — Basic Structure and Harmony (1973 onwards)

Kesavananda Bharati (1973) — Parliament can amend Fundamental Rights but cannot destroy the basic structure. The balance between FRs and DPSPs is itself a basic feature.

⚖ Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India
AIR 1980 SC 1789
Held
The harmony and balance between Fundamental Rights and DPSPs is a basic feature of the Constitution. The 42nd Amendment's extension of Art. 31C to all DPSPs was struck down. Parliament cannot give absolute primacy to DPSPs over all Fundamental Rights.

3. Current Position

Today, the relationship between FRs and DPSPs is one of harmony and complementarity. DPSPs are used to expand the scope of Art. 21. Laws that reasonably restrict FRs to promote DPSP objectives are upheld. Both must be read together as parts of an integrated scheme.

Fundamental Duties (Art. 51A)

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Introduction

Fundamental Duties were not part of the original Constitution. Added by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976 on the recommendation of the Swaran Singh Committee. Originally 10 duties. The 86th Amendment, 2002 added the 11th duty. Contained in Part IV-A (Article 51A).

2. List of Fundamental Duties

  1. Abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals, National Flag and National Anthem.
  2. Cherish and follow noble ideals of the freedom struggle.
  3. Uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India.
  4. Defend the country and render national service when called upon.
  5. Promote harmony and brotherhood — transcending religious, linguistic, regional diversities.
  6. Value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture.
  7. Protect and improve the natural environment — forests, lakes, rivers, wildlife.
  8. Develop scientific temper, humanism, and spirit of inquiry and reform.
  9. Safeguard public property and abjure violence.
  10. Strive towards excellence in individual and collective activity.
  11. (Added 2002) Parent/guardian must provide education to child aged 6–14 years.

3. Nature and Enforceability

Fundamental Duties are NOT justiciable — cannot be directly enforced by courts. However, Parliament can enact laws to enforce them. E.g., the Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act, 1971 punishes disrespect to the National Flag and National Anthem.

4. Significance

  • Courts can use Fundamental Duties to determine reasonableness of restrictions on Fundamental Rights.
  • A law enforcing a Fundamental Duty is more likely to be upheld as a reasonable restriction.
  • In Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (1986) — the SC considered the duty to respect the National Anthem.
  • They promote civic consciousness and national values.

The President of India

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Constitutional Position (Art. 52–62)

The President is the constitutional head of India. Real executive power lies with the Prime Minister and Council of Ministers. The President acts on their aid and advice (Art. 74). The President is both the head of State and head of the Union Executive in a formal sense.

2. Qualifications, Election and Impeachment

  • Citizen of India, completed 35 years, qualified for Lok Sabha membership, no office of profit.
  • Elected indirectly by an Electoral College — elected members of both Houses of Parliament + elected members of State Legislative Assemblies. By Single Transferable Vote.
  • Five-year term. Removed by impeachment (Art. 61) — 2/3 majority of total membership of one House, investigated and passed by the other House with same majority.

3. Powers of the President

Executive Powers (Art. 53, 77)

  • All executive actions in the name of the President.
  • Appointment of PM, Ministers, CJI, Judges, Governors, AG, CAG, UPSC members, Election Commission, etc.
  • Supreme Commander of Armed Forces.

Legislative Powers

  • Summons, prorogues Parliament; dissolves Lok Sabha. Nominates 12 members to Rajya Sabha.
  • Assent to bills — can assent, withhold, or return ordinary bills. Cannot return Money Bills.
  • Promulgates Ordinances (Art. 123) when Parliament is not in session.

Judicial Powers (Art. 72)

PowerMeaning
PardonComplete absolution of sentence and conviction
ReprieveTemporary suspension of sentence
RespiteReduction of sentence due to special circumstances
RemissionReduction of sentence without changing its character
CommutationSubstitution of lesser punishment for a greater one

Emergency Powers

  • Art. 352: National Emergency — war, external aggression, armed rebellion.
  • Art. 356: President's Rule — breakdown of constitutional machinery in state.
  • Art. 360: Financial Emergency.

4. Vice President (Art. 63–71)

India's second highest constitutional office. Elected by members of BOTH Houses of Parliament (including nominated members) — by Single Transferable Vote. Ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha. Acts as President when office is vacant. Five-year term.

Union Legislature — Parliament

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Parliament — Composition (Art. 79)

Parliament consists of: (i) The President; (ii) Rajya Sabha (Council of States); (iii) Lok Sabha (House of the People). Also includes the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister.

2. Council of Ministers (Art. 74–75)

Art. 74: There shall be a Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at the head to aid and advise the President. The President shall act in accordance with such advice.

Art. 75: PM appointed by the President. Other Ministers appointed on the advice of PM. Council of Ministers collectively responsible to Lok Sabha. Ministers hold office during the pleasure of the President.

Prime Minister's Role: Head of the Council of Ministers, chairs Cabinet meetings, chief link between President and Cabinet, coordinates between Ministries, can recommend dissolution of Lok Sabha.

3. Rajya Sabha (Council of States)

FeatureDetail
Maximum Strength250 (238 elected + 12 nominated by President)
ElectionElected by elected members of State Legislative Assemblies — Proportional Representation (STV)
TermPermanent House — not dissolved. 1/3rd retire every 2 years
QualificationCitizen of India, 30 years, registered voter in any state
Presiding OfficerVice President (ex-officio Chairman)

4. Lok Sabha (House of the People)

FeatureDetail
Maximum Strength552 (530 states + 20 UTs + 2 Anglo-Indians — abolished 2020)
ElectionDirect election by universal adult franchise
Term5 years (can be dissolved by President on PM's advice)
QualificationCitizen of India, 25 years, registered voter in any constituency
Presiding OfficerSpeaker (elected from members)

5. Legislative Procedure

Ordinary Bills

Three readings in each House. After both Houses pass it, goes to President for assent. If disagreement — Joint Sitting (Art. 108) presided by Speaker of Lok Sabha, bill passed by simple majority.

Money Bills (Art. 110)

  • Can be introduced only in Lok Sabha with prior Presidential recommendation.
  • Rajya Sabha has 14 days to return it with recommendations only (cannot amend or reject).
  • Lok Sabha is NOT bound to accept recommendations. No Joint Sitting for Money Bills.

Constitutional Amendment Bills (Art. 368)

Special majority — 2/3rd of members present and voting + majority of total membership in both Houses separately. Certain provisions need ratification by at least half of state legislatures. No Joint Sitting for constitutional amendments.

Union Judiciary — Supreme Court

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Establishment and Composition (Art. 124)

The Supreme Court of India was established on 26 January 1950. Currently has 1 Chief Justice + 33 other Judges (total 34). Parliament has power to increase the number. The Collegium System operates for judicial appointments — CJI and 4 senior-most SC judges recommend names.

2. Qualifications and Tenure

  • Citizen of India.
  • HC judge for at least 5 years; OR HC advocate for at least 10 years; OR distinguished jurist in the President's opinion.
  • Retirement at 65 years. Removal by impeachment only (special majority of Parliament).

3. Jurisdiction of the Supreme Court

(a) Original Jurisdiction (Art. 131)

Exclusive jurisdiction in disputes: between Union and States, between Union + States on one side vs. other States, and between two or more States.

(b) Writ Jurisdiction (Art. 32)

Issues five writs for enforcement of Fundamental Rights.

(c) Appellate Jurisdiction

  • Constitutional (Art. 132): Substantial question of law as to interpretation of the Constitution.
  • Civil (Art. 133): HC certifies substantial question of law of general importance.
  • Criminal (Art. 134): HC reversed acquittal, sentenced to death, or certified fitness.
  • Special Leave Petition (Art. 136): SC may grant special leave to appeal from any court/tribunal in India.

(d) Advisory Jurisdiction (Art. 143)

President may refer any question of public importance to SC for its opinion. Opinion is not binding.

(e) Review Jurisdiction (Art. 137)

SC can review its own judgments. Review petition within 30 days. Heard by same bench in chambers.

4. Law Declared by SC — Binding on All Courts (Art. 141)

The law declared by the Supreme Court shall be binding on all courts within India.

⚖ Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Assn. v. Union of India (NJAC Case)
AIR 2016 SC 117
Held
Judicial independence is a basic feature of the Constitution. The NJAC Act and 99th Amendment were struck down. Including the executive in judicial appointments violates judicial independence. Collegium system continues.

State Executive — The Governor

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Constitutional Position (Art. 153)

The Governor is the constitutional head of the state. Appointed by the President and holds office during the President's pleasure (Art. 156). Dual role: (i) Constitutional head of state — acts on advice of Council of Ministers; (ii) Agent of the Centre — represents Union interests in the state.

2. Qualifications (Art. 157)

  • Citizen of India, completed 35 years, not a member of Parliament or State Legislature, no office of profit.

3. Powers of the Governor

Executive Powers

  • All executive actions in the name of the Governor.
  • Appointment of CM, other Ministers, Advocate General, State PSC members, etc.

Legislative Powers

  • Summons, prorogues State Legislature; dissolves Vidhan Sabha.
  • Nominates 1/6th of Vidhan Parishad members (where it exists).
  • Assent to state bills — can assent, withhold, return, or reserve for Presidential consideration.
  • Promulgates Ordinances (Art. 213) when State Legislature is not in session.

Judicial Powers (Art. 161)

Power to grant pardons for offences against state laws (similar to Presidential power under Art. 72 but limited to state laws).

Emergency Powers

Art. 356: If Governor reports failure of constitutional machinery, President's Rule can be proclaimed.

4. Discretionary Powers

  • Reservation of a state bill for Presidential assent (Art. 200).
  • Appointment of CM when no party has a clear majority.
  • Dismissal of a government that has lost the confidence of the legislature.
  • Reporting to President on breakdown of constitutional machinery.
  • Seeking information from CM about state administration (Art. 167).
⚖ B.P. Singhal v. Union of India
AIR 2010 SC 3026
Held
Governors cannot be removed arbitrarily or on political grounds. The power under Art. 156(1) is not absolute. Purely political removal is impermissible.

State Legislature — Vidhan Sabha & Vidhan Parishad

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Organisation (Art. 168)

State Legislature consists of: (i) Governor; (ii) Vidhan Sabha (mandatory — all states); (iii) Vidhan Parishad (optional — 6 states: Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Bihar, Uttar Pradesh).

2. Vidhan Sabha — State Legislative Assembly (Art. 170)

  • Maximum: 500 members; Minimum: 60 members. Directly elected by universal adult franchise.
  • Term: 5 years. Can be dissolved earlier by Governor or under President's Rule.
  • Presiding Officer: Speaker (elected from members).
  • Qualification: Citizen of India, minimum 25 years, registered voter in the state.

3. Vidhan Parishad — State Legislative Council (Art. 171)

ProportionElected/Nominated By
1/3rdMembers of Vidhan Sabha
1/3rdLocal bodies (municipalities, district boards)
1/12thGraduates (3+ years standing) of the state
1/12thTeachers of secondary schools and above
1/6thNominated by Governor (art, science, literature, social service)

Maximum: 1/3rd of total Vidhan Sabha members. Minimum: 40. Permanent House — 1/3rd retire every 2 years.

4. Powers of Vidhan Parishad

Weaker than Vidhan Sabha. On ordinary bills, can delay up to 4 months. No joint sitting for state legislatures — Vidhan Sabha can override through re-passage. Money Bills cannot be introduced in Vidhan Parishad — must return within 14 days with recommendations only.

5. Creation and Abolition (Art. 169)

Parliament can create or abolish a Vidhan Parishad for a state if the Vidhan Sabha passes a resolution with 2/3rd of members present and voting + majority of total membership.

⚖ Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh
AIR 2016 SC 3428
Held
The Governor cannot summon the Assembly session on his own — must act on advice of Council of Ministers. Imposition of President's Rule held unconstitutional. Governor's discretionary power is limited and cannot destabilise a democratically elected government.

State Judiciary — High Courts

Constitutional Law – I  |  Legalcraft Study Notes  |  DAVV Indore

1. Establishment (Art. 214)

Every state shall have a High Court. India currently has 25 High Courts. Parliament can establish a common High Court for two or more states (e.g., Punjab and Haryana High Court).

2. Appointment and Tenure

  • Judges appointed by the President after consultation with the CJI and the Governor of the state. Collegium System applies.
  • Qualifications: Citizen of India; HC judge for at least 10 years, OR HC advocate for at least 10 years.
  • Retirement at 62 years (compared to 65 for SC judges). Removal only by Parliament through impeachment.

3. Jurisdiction of High Courts

(a) Original Jurisdiction

Revenue/tax matters (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras originally), matrimonial disputes, admiralty cases, company law matters.

(b) Writ Jurisdiction (Art. 226)

High Courts have wider writ jurisdiction than the Supreme Court. Issues writs for: (i) Enforcement of Fundamental Rights; AND (ii) Any other purpose — any legal right, statutory right, etc.

(c) Appellate Jurisdiction

Civil cases from District Courts, Criminal cases from Sessions Courts, and Revenue and other specialised courts.

(d) Supervisory Jurisdiction (Art. 227)

Every High Court has supervisory jurisdiction over all courts and tribunals within its territorial jurisdiction. Can call for records, give directions, ensure proper administration of justice.

(e) Court of Record

Judgments recorded for perpetuity with evidentiary value. Power to punish for contempt of court.

4. Art. 226 vs. Art. 32

BasisArt. 226 (High Court)Art. 32 (Supreme Court)
ScopeFR + any other legal rightOnly Fundamental Rights
TerritoryWithin HC's jurisdictionAll of India
NatureConstitutional rightFundamental Right itself
SuspensionCannot be fully suspendedCan be suspended during Emergency (Art. 359)
⚖ L. Chandrakumar v. Union of India
AIR 1997 SC 1125
Held
The power of judicial review vested in High Courts under Art. 226/227 is a part of the basic structure of the Constitution. Clauses ousting HC jurisdiction are unconstitutional.
